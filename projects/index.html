Projects

Generating neural diversity

Across animals, developing nervous systems produce a remarkable range of neural cell types. This diversity arises from stem cells that interpret and integrate positional information, developmental time, and local signals to generate distinct cell identities. How these different layers of information are integrated within progenitors, and how they shape the assembly of functional circuits, remains a central question in neurobiology.

We study this process across multiple systems, asking how spatial cues, temporal programs, and chromatin landscapes interact to produce specific neuronal outcomes. Depending on the organism or context, we pair genetic and developmental approaches with single-cell and epigenomic methods to map lineage decisions and the regulatory logic that drives them.

Blood feeding in mosquitoes

Animals constantly adjust what they eat and when, balancing external cues with their internal needs. Mosquitoes offer a striking example of this behavioural plasticity. Both male and female mosquitoes feed on nectar or sap, but the females also take blood-meals for egg development. Their feeding decision - sap or blood-meal - depends on their physiological state, reproductive timing, and the environmental context. We study how these shifting internal states are represented at the molecular and circuit levels. Using a combination of behavioural approaches and modern genomic and imaging tools, we aim to reveal how internal state reshapes sensory processing and decision-making in the mosquito brain.

Chemical ecology of mosquitoes

Insects navigate their world through chemical cues. Plants, hosts, water bodies, and microbial communities all release signals that shape insect behaviour: what they feed on, where they lay their eggs, and how they mate.

We take such an ecological view of mosquito biology and behaviour, asking how different landscapes, seasons, and species shape the cues available to mosquitoes, and how they in turn use those cues to find food, choose habitats, and make decisions across their life cycle. By pairing field ecology with chemical and behavioural assays, we aim to identify the signals that matter most to mosquitoes and understand why. Because many effective intervention strategies are ultimately grounded in chemical ecology, studying these interactions offers a path toward approaches that align with mosquito biology and ecology rather than working agnostic of them.

Collaborators: Saskya Van Nouhuys, Amey Redkar

Kinematics of larval swim behaviours

Mosquito larvae move in surprisingly characteristic ways, shaped by their morphology, sensory world, and the aquatic environments they inhabit. Their swimming involves coordinated bouts, pauses, and turns, and these patterns differ across groups in ways that reflect both ecology and evolution. We study these movement signatures by tracking how larvae accelerate, reorient, and explore their surroundings in response to cues. Work of this kind can also form the foundation for developing simple, accessible tools that use movement patterns to distinguish larvae in the field.

Collaborators: Subhasis Ray, TCG Crest

Custom tools for gene expression, functional genomics

Many of the systems we work in require approaches that either don’t yet exist, are too expensive, or aren’t easily accessible. We adapt and develop methods for visualising, measuring, and manipulating gene expression across species. These “home-brew” tools allow us to ask mechanistic questions in emerging models and to scale up exploratory work where standard reagent pipelines fall short.

Sitara Oceans

Gaining evolutionary insights into brain development requires studying organisms that sit at key phylogenetic positions across the tree of life. We want to develop model systems from early diverging animals with complex nervous systems. In collaboration with Dr. Haimanti Biswas at the NIO, we’re exploring the Indian marine environment for the biodiversity it offers. This early groundwork helps to identify and establish appropriate species for future evolutionary comparisons.

Collaborators: Haimanti Biswas, NIO and Farah Ishtiaq
